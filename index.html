How It All Works

[[ What Is This? ]]

This interactive graph visualizes musical harmony-the science of why some notes sound better together.

  • The cool-colored "valleys" on the graphs show you which note combinations sound pleasant, or consonant.
  • The warm-colored "peaks" show which combinations sound less pleasant, or dissonant.

It's all based on how the hidden frequencies in every natural sound—called overtones—interact with each other.

[[ The Control Deck ]]

A quick guide to the lab controls. The highlighted ones have the biggest impact.

Base Frequency:
The starting note's pitch in Hertz (Hz). A higher value means a higher pitch. An octave is twice as high as its base frequency. "Middle C" is 261.63 Hz.
Overtones:
The higher pitched, hidden tones that give a sound its character. More overtones create a richer sound and a more complex graph (zero overtones is a pure sine wave).
Octaves:
The range of notes to display on the graph's axes, from one octave up to four. Recommended: 1 or 2.
Dissonance Model:
The equation used to calculate roughness. Sethares is the standard model. Dillon is more sensitive and amplifies the harshest-sounding frequency interactions.
Instrument Timbre:
Presets for overtone patterns. Instruments sound different because their overtones have unique strengths. A piano's pattern differs from a bell's, changing the graph.
Tones:
Sets how many notes are compared against the base note, changing the graph's dimensions: 2=2D curve, 3=3D surface, 4=4D scatter plot.
Tuning System:
There are different ways to tune an instrument, each with their own pros and cons. This changes how the musical scale is tuned, which determines the exact placement of the labeled dots (the chords and intervals) on the dissonance curve.
Chart Type:
Selects how the data is visualized. Each Tone setting offers two different chart types to view the same data from a new perspective. See below for more.

[[ Reading the Scans ]]

The graph is your map. You can zoom in and out, rotate (if the chart is 3D), and click to make sound. Look for the blue valleys and red peaks. Blue = Harmony. Red = Tension.

>> 2 Tones (Dyads)

Line Curve

In a nutshell: Valleys are harmonious intervals (an interval is a combination of two notes/frequencies). The x-axis is the pitch relationship of the 2nd tone to the base frequency. The y-axis is the amount of dissonance or 'roughness' of the combined tones. The shape of this curve changes as overtones increase/decrease in number and in strength.

The labeled dots on the curve represent commonly used intervals in music (e.g., perfect fifth) - different tunings place the dot in different places. In other words - there is more than one way to tune an instrument. Despite the different tuning methods, notice how the dots tend to fall more in valleys than peaks. This is where 'notes', the building blocks of music, come from - the valleys of consonance.

Polar Area

In a nutshell: The same data wrapped in a circle. Closer to the center is more harmonious. Different tuning methods give you a different "ticks" to the "musical clock" or scale. Notice how some tuning methods have more equal spacing than others, making it easier/harder to place music in different "keys" (base frequencies). Most music today uses 12-TET due to the ability to change keys (base frequencies) without needing to retune the instrument, but that was not always the case.

>> 3 Tones (Triads)

3D Surface

In a nutshell: A landscape of harmony from chords (a combination of three or more notes/frequencies). The deep blue "canyons" are stable chords like Major and Minor. Notice how most the labeled dots (chords) typically correspond to different valleys.

2D Heatmap

In a nutshell: A top-down view of the 3D surface. The faint light streaks are your "highways to harmony." These "roads" are directly influenced by the number and strength of overtones. Different tuning methods will place the labeled dots on different parts along these pathways and intersections.

>> 4 Tones (Tetrads)

3D Scatter

In a nutshell: A 4D model where the x, y, and z axis represent the the interval between the base note and the second, third, and fourth notes of a chord. The size and color of the labeled dots show the harmony of the chord—bigger, bluer dots are more consonant.

3D Spherical Model

In a nutshell: An abstract map of harmony. This view organizes chords based on their geometric relationships rather than their exact frequency ratios.

[[ The Data Readout ]]

The table below the graph lists all standard musical chords and intervals. Use it to quickly find and listen to the most important sounds. Here's what each column means:

Name:
The common musical name for the chord or interval (e.g., "Major Triad").
Ratios:
The mathematical pitch relationship of each note to the base frequency.
Piano Notes:
The notes of the chord as they would appear on a standard piano.
MIDI Notes:
A numeric representation of the notes, where 60 is Middle C.
Roughness:
The calculated dissonance score. Click this header to sort from most harmonious (lowest number) to most dissonant (highest).
Sound:
Click the Play button to hear the chord.
